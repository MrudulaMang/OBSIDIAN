Yes, but not by following the same path as a fresh graduate or a typical 2–5 year engineer.

Based on what you've told me before:

- You have a programming foundation in C, C++, Java, and Oracle.
- You're building AWS, Terraform, Linux, and DevOps skills.
- You enjoy debugging, systems thinking, root-cause analysis, and reliability work.
- You have a significant career gap.

The career gap is the biggest obstacle, not your technical ability.

A direct jump from your current position to a FAANG role would be difficult because FAANG recruiters tend to filter heavily on recent experience. However, that does not mean FAANG is unreachable.

A more realistic path is:

1. Become genuinely strong in Linux, AWS, Terraform, Kubernetes, CI/CD, observability, and troubleshooting.
2. Land a DevOps, SRE, Cloud Engineer, or Platform Engineer role at a smaller company.
3. Build 2–3 years of recent production experience.
4. Target larger companies afterward.

The opportunity cost of chasing FAANG immediately is that you may spend years grinding interview questions while lacking the recent experience recruiters want.

What improves your odds dramatically:

- Deep Linux troubleshooting skills
- Strong system design understanding
- Real cloud projects
- Production incident experience
- Kubernetes expertise
- Coding ability (Python or Go helps)
- LeetCode for interviews

What hurts your odds:

- Tool-only knowledge
- Certificate collecting
- No recent employment history
- No demonstrable projects

If I were evaluating your situation as a hiring manager, I would not position you as "another Terraform engineer."

I would position you as:

> Systems programmer → Cloud/SRE engineer specializing in debugging, reliability, and distributed systems.

That narrative is much stronger and more aligned with your background.

The more important question is not "Can I get into FAANG?"

The question is:

**Can you become the kind of engineer who can keep a large-scale production system alive during a major outage?**

If the answer becomes yes, FAANG-level opportunities become a consequence rather than the primary goal.
