A CTO is the Chief Technology Officer.

They are responsible for the company’s overall technology direction—what gets built, how it gets built, and whether the technology supports business goals.

Think of the rough distinction like this:

- CEO → “Where is the company going?”
- CTO → “How will technology help us get there?”
- CIO → “How do internal systems run efficiently?”
- VP Engineering / Engineering Director → “How do engineering teams execute delivery?”

A CTO focuses on things like:

- technology strategy
- architecture decisions
- engineering standards
- platform scalability
- security and reliability direction
- build vs buy decisions
- technical hiring standards
- long-term innovation

In startups, the CTO may still write code and design systems.

In large companies, the CTO is usually more strategic—less coding, more decisions, influence, and organizational architecture.

For someone in SRE/Platform/Architecture, the path can evolve like:

Engineer → Senior Engineer → Staff/Principal → Engineering Manager / Architect → Director → VP → CTO

But many strong technical people never become CTOs because CTO is partly a business leadership role, not just technical excellence.

Important distinction:

A great debugger is not automatically a great CTO.

A CTO must align technology with money, people, and business risk.

That is a different game.
